Can Chemo Make You Lose Your Hair

Written by Luffy Sep 12, 2023 Â· 7 min read

If you or someone you know is going through chemotherapy, one of the most common concerns is whether or not chemo can make you lose your hair. Unfortunately, the answer is yes. Hair loss is a common side effect of chemotherapy, and it can be a difficult and emotional experience for many people.

What is chemotherapy?

Chemotherapy is a type of cancer treatment that uses drugs to kill cancer cells. These drugs are usually given intravenously, but they can also be taken orally or applied topically. The goal of chemotherapy is to destroy cancer cells, but unfortunately, it can also damage healthy cells in the body.

How does chemotherapy cause hair loss?

Chemotherapy drugs target fast-growing cells in the body, including cancer cells but also hair follicles. When hair follicles are damaged by chemotherapy, the hair falls out. Hair loss from chemotherapy can occur anywhere on the body, including the scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes, and pubic area.

When does hair loss from chemotherapy occur?

Hair loss from chemotherapy usually begins within 2-4 weeks of starting treatment. The amount of hair loss can vary depending on the type and dose of chemotherapy drugs used. Some people may only experience mild thinning, while others may lose all of their hair.

Is hair loss from chemotherapy permanent?

Hair loss from chemotherapy is usually temporary, and hair will begin to grow back once treatment is complete. However, it may take several months for hair to grow back to its normal length and thickness.

How can I prepare for hair loss from chemotherapy?

If you know that you will be undergoing chemotherapy, it is a good idea to prepare for hair loss in advance. Here are some tips:

1. Cut your hair short

Cutting your hair short before starting chemotherapy can make the hair loss less noticeable.

2. Consider a wig or hairpiece

Many people choose to wear a wig or hairpiece during chemotherapy to help them feel more comfortable and confident.

3. Protect your scalp

During chemotherapy, your scalp may be more sensitive than usual. Wearing a hat or scarf can help protect your scalp from the sun and cold.

4. Be gentle with your hair

Avoid using harsh chemicals or heat styling tools on your hair during chemotherapy. Use a gentle shampoo and conditioner, and avoid brushing your hair when it is wet.

How can I care for my hair during chemotherapy?

Even though you may lose your hair during chemotherapy, it is still important to take care of your scalp and any remaining hair. Here are some tips:

1. Use a gentle shampoo and conditioner

Choose a shampoo and conditioner that is gentle and free of harsh chemicals.

2. Avoid heat styling tools

Avoid using heat styling tools like blow dryers, curling irons, and straighteners on your hair.

3. Protect your scalp from the sun

Wear a hat or scarf when you are outside to protect your scalp from the sun.

4. Be gentle when brushing or combing your hair

Use a wide-toothed comb or a soft-bristled brush to gently detangle your hair.

FAQ

Q: Will all chemotherapy drugs cause hair loss?

A: No, not all chemotherapy drugs cause hair loss. The likelihood and severity of hair loss can vary depending on the type and dose of chemotherapy drugs used.

Q: Is there anything I can do to prevent hair loss from chemotherapy?

A: Unfortunately, there is no way to prevent hair loss from chemotherapy. However, there are some treatments, such as scalp cooling, that may reduce the amount of hair loss.

Q: Will my hair grow back after chemotherapy?

A: Yes, in most cases hair will grow back after chemotherapy. However, it may take several months for hair to grow back to its normal length and thickness.

Conclusion

Hair loss from chemotherapy can be a difficult and emotional experience, but it is usually temporary. Preparing for hair loss and taking care of your scalp and any remaining hair can help make the experience more manageable. If you have any concerns about hair loss or other side effects of chemotherapy, talk to your healthcare provider.
